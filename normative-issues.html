Concept Guide

What Are Normative Issues, Normativity, and Moral Issues?

This page uses the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entries on normativity in metaethics and the definition of morality to clarify key terms used in this project.

What Is A Normative Issue?

A normative issue asks what should be done, believed, valued, or avoided, not just what is factually true. In metaethics, normative discourse includes moral judgments (for example, what is right or wrong), epistemic judgments (what one ought to believe), and aesthetic judgments (what is beautiful or ugly).

So, a normative issue is broader than a strictly moral issue: it covers any domain where reasons, standards, and "ought" claims are in play.

What Is Normativity?

Normativity is the structure of standards and reasons that make some judgments or actions justified and others mistaken. The metaethical question is not only which standards we use, but what makes them authoritative: are normative truths objective, mind-dependent, reducible to non-normative facts, or irreducible?

This is why debates about reasons, value, motivation, and objectivity are central to normativity. They ask what kind of thing a normative fact is, and how human deliberation can track it.

What Counts As A Moral Issue?

The SEP entry on morality stresses that there is no single uncontested definition. One common use is descriptive: "morality" means the code actually accepted by a person or group. Another common use is normative: "morality" means the standards that all rational people would have reason to accept under suitably impartial conditions.

On this site, we treat moral issues as a major subset of normative issues, especially where harms, obligations, fairness, rights, and interpersonal treatment are concerned.

How Normativity Uses These Terms

  • Normative issue: any issue involving reasons or "ought" judgments.
  • Moral issue: normative issues about right/wrong action and interpersonal obligations.
  • Normativity: the underlying space of reasons, standards, and evaluative truth-claims.

The practical goal is not to treat disagreement as noise, but to use structured deliberation (including wide reflective equilibrium) to improve coherence between judgments, principles, and background theories.
