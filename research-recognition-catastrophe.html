Recognition of Moral Catastrophe

Societies can normalize severe wrongs for long periods. This essay asks how institutions might identify candidate moral catastrophes earlier without pretending that contested claims have already been proven.

Core claim

If previous societies could live inside large moral failures while feeling ordinary from the inside, current societies should treat their own confidence with caution. The right response is not moral panic. It is better detection, better disagreement, and more reversible institutions.

Recognition signals

  • Large numbers of affected beings or people with weak political voice.
  • High dependence on inherited practice, convenience, or economic normalization.
  • Disagreement where one side has strong incentives not to look closely.
  • Evidence that ordinary framing hides the morally relevant description.

What public institutions should do

Institutions should create space for candidate recognition without converting every candidate into a verdict. Useful outputs include clearer issue decomposition, better exposure to neglected perspectives, calibrated uncertainty, and low-regret safeguards.

Known limits

  • False positives are possible and can cause real harm.
  • Historical analogy can be overused or rhetorically weaponized.
  • Powerful actors may selectively promote candidate catastrophes that serve them.
  • Recognition systems need privacy, correction, and appeal paths before action pressure.
