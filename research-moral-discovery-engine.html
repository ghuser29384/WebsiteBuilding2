Moral Discovery Engine

A working draft on institutions that decompose moral disagreement, route subclaims through appropriate feedback systems, publish revisions, and preserve reversibility while moral inquiry improves.

Core claim

A practical path to moral discovery is a governed stack rather than a single method. Philosophical analysis clarifies claims. Empirical methods test consequences where possible. Structured deliberation exposes objections and lived perspectives. Public revision logs make corrections visible.

Institutional stack

  1. Decompose disputes. Identify empirical claims, conceptual claims, moral premises, and decision constraints.
  2. Route subclaims. Use forecasting, experiments, argument mapping, WRE review, or deliberation depending on the claim type.
  3. Publish uncertainty. Show where confidence changes, what evidence mattered, and what remains unresolved.
  4. Keep outputs corrigible. Use versioning, review, revocation, and sunset rules before any action-facing policy hardens.

Public judgment protocol

The public should not be asked to "trust the model" or "trust the market." New arguments should be judged by visible method quality: what was tested, what failed, what changed, who reviewed it, and whether alternative framings received fair treatment.

Known limits

  • Moral claims often lack decisive feedback loops.
  • Scores and markets can become targets for status competition.
  • Institutional agenda-setting can hide neglected candidate wrongs.
  • Public uptake requires diverse messengers and visible correction, not only better arguments.
